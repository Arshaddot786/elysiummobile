Chapter One

10 to 12 years.
18-hour days. Every day.
He was born to build.

This story does not start with the fall. It starts much earlier — with a boy who had no connections, no inheritance, no shortcut, and the most dangerous kind of hunger: the hunger of someone who genuinely believes they can build something real with their own two hands.

For 10 to 12 years, Arshad Noor worked 14 to 18 hours a day. Not occasionally. Not during crunch periods. Every single day. He tried small businesses. Side hustles. Always searching — what is the thing I am meant to build? What is the work that is mine? He tried, failed, tried again, failed again, got up again. While most people slept, he was working. While most people rested on weekends, he was in the office.

When he built his ecommerce agency — Ecom Hustlers Capital — he ran two offices simultaneously. He managed shifts. He matched US hours. There were weeks he did not go home at all — he slept a few hours on the office floor and kept working. He built a team of thirty people. He generated $2 million in client revenue in 2022. He was teaching, speaking at universities, starting every session with Bismillah because faith was never a decoration for him — it was the engine.

He named his convertible car Bubbly. He drove with the top down on the streets of Karachi. He sat on the hood and looked at the city skyline. He stood next to the biggest names in Pakistani ecommerce. He had earned every single bit of it — with his body, his sleep, his years.

December 31, 2022 — The 10M goal. The team. Everything felt possible.
Ecom Hustlers Capital — built with 18-hour days for over a decade.
Bubbly — the car he earned, the freedom he built.
Karachi streets — the peak years.
With Usman Chudtai — ecommercewala. The summit.
Teaching Pakistan's next generation of entrepreneurs.
"Starting with the name of Allah" — every single session.
"I am an Entrepreneur by Birth. I will lose more than I win — but when I win, Inshallah, I will Win Bigger with the Help of Allah."
— Arshad Noor

Chapter Two

There was May.
His love of life.
His lifeline.

Before everything fell, there was May. She was not just a person he loved — she was his lifeline. The person who made the 18-hour days feel like they had a destination. The person he was building toward.

They had planned their marriage. In Turkey. They had bought the tickets — $2,400 USD — and those tickets represented everything that mattered. A future. A home. A reason beyond the business. He was not just an entrepreneur chasing a goal. He was a man in love, planning a life.

Those tickets became part of the loss. Not the biggest loss in dollars — but perhaps the one that cost the most. Because May was more than a relationship. She was the anchor that, when she was gone, left him completely exposed to everything that came next. He believes — and he says this clearly — that if May had still been in his life, he would not have fallen for the next two scams. The loneliness that followed her absence is what opened the door. And the scammers walked right through it.

Chapter Three

Three scams.
$150,000 gone.
The first one wore a human face.

The collapse did not come all at once. It came in three blows — each one hitting before the last had healed. And the first blow was not a stranger on a screen. It was someone who knew exactly who he was.

She did not find him by accident. She came in through his own kindness — she asked him for a job for her sister, and he gave it to her. From inside his own office, she watched. She learned his assets, his lifestyle, the seminars, the meetups, the security around him. She watched a young man rise faster than anyone around him — and instead of admiring it from a distance, she decided to own it.

Over six months, what started as admiration became total psychological control. She studied everything — his faith, his loneliness, his trust. She knew he was deeply religious, so she made herself appear devout too. She told him an old man had once predicted, years before they met, that her husband's name would begin with the letter A — and that meeting him felt like destiny fulfilling itself. At the time, with her tone, her tears, her fabricated devotion, every word felt completely real.

Then came the manipulation. She invented stories of other men "blackmailing" her — men who, in reality, she had simply been involved with — and convinced him these innocent people were threatening her life. She love-bombed him publicly with painful, dramatic posts. And when he hesitated, she escalated to outright blackmail: marry her, or she would take poison. Marry her, or she would kill herself. She posted these threats to her WhatsApp status — calculated, public, designed to trap him in front of an audience he could not explain himself to.

She fabricated a story that she had been kidnapped and raped as a child because her father was a political worker connected to the men who took her. She said her own father never cared for her, never gave her money, because he hated her mother — and she resembled her mother, so he hated her too. He believed every word. He found out much later that the reality was different — she had been in and out of relationships for years, blackmailing people, until some of it finally caught up with her through her own family.

She told him to leave his own house — to become "homeless in her memory" — while she secretly ran off with another man she was already involved with. She told him that man was physically abusing her, forcing himself on her, and that she had no one left to save her. For two years, he could not walk to the local market near his own home — a market his mother had sent him to a hundred times before — because the memory of her was tied to those streets. He sent his brother or his cousins instead.

By the time it ended, she had taken six months to completely destroy a man and the business he had spent over a decade building.

"I met a real evil in this life. Whatever I write, I cannot capture how dramatic and painful the sequence of this story truly was. To this day — three years later — if I talk about her, or even see a woman who reminds me of her, my mind and body hurt for a day or two. I believe, with everything in me, that she has a pure evil soul. I wish her hell in this dunya and in the Akhira. And I am waiting for the day, inshallah, when she exposes herself to the world with her own words."

The Three Years Nobody Saw
Chapter Five

Extreme loneliness.
Drugs to get sleep.
Three suicide attempts.

What followed the scams is the part of the story that most founder stories never tell. Because it is not clean. It is not inspiring in the conventional sense. It is just — the truth of what happens to a human being when everything is taken and there is nobody beside them.

He could not tell his family. He is their only hope. Their provider. Their survival in a hard world. They had already suffered greatly — and he could not add this weight to theirs. They would see it on his face eventually. But the full extent — he carried that alone. Every single day. He showed the world he was fine. Inside — he was disappearing.

The weight became physical
Same man. Different eyes.

The loneliness was the most brutal part. Not the money. Not the failure. The loneliness. He would pick up his phone and open WhatsApp — not to send a message, but just to check. Did anyone reach out? Did anyone feel him? Did anyone notice? Did anyone want to help him heal? The answer, day after day, was no. He was posting painful things — not as content — but as the cries of someone who was hurting and had nowhere to put it. And the silence that came back was its own kind of violence.

The loneliness that the loss of May created is what he believes made him vulnerable to the second and third scam. When you are that alone — that desperate for connection, for someone who sees you — you become reachable by anyone who offers warmth. Even if that warmth is manufactured. Even if it is a trap.

"I would check WhatsApp again and again — did someone talk to me? Did someone feel me? Did someone want to help me heal? I was in complete darkness. Complete loneliness. And I have suffered, and I don't want that loneliness for anyone."

The Substances

He needed sleep.
His body had completely broken.

There came a point where his body could not rest. Could not shut down. Could not find even a few hours of sleep without help. The panic attacks came without warning — dozens of them. His heart was failing under the weight. His legs stopped working — a falij attack that left him unable to walk for months.

And so he fell into substances. Not by choice in the way most people imagine that word. But because a broken body, in unbearable pain, alone, with no help, will eventually reach for anything that offers even a few hours of rest. He would resist. He would fight it. He would stop for a few days, a few weeks sometimes. And then the sleeplessness and the pain would come back harder, and he would fall again. And then wake up and hate himself for it. And the cycle would begin again.

He did not eat for days. Did not drink water for days. He lost himself in a foreign country — wandering, completely disoriented, sleeping in places no one should sleep. An ambulance found him once and took him back to his hotel. Army men in another country saw him crying so badly — completely uncontrolled — that they stopped, gave him water, and stayed with him until he could breathe again. He was that far gone. That completely alone.

"I believe I cried in those three years more than I have in my entire life before them. So much that I don't think I have tears left. I had a major heart attack. My legs stopped working — I couldn't walk for months. Dozens of panic attacks. And I was doing all of this while still being my family's only hope."

Chapter Six

At his lowest —
revenge was
right there.

After three suicide attempts. After a heart attack. After months unable to walk. After years of checking WhatsApp hoping someone would feel him. After the drugs, the panic attacks, the nights in foreign countries lost and alone — there was one more test. And this one did not come as a feeling. It came as a real, specific offer.

People he knew personally — close to his own offices, people who had shared tea with him, sat across from him — were already running real operations. Not abstractions. Specific businesses: fake call centers, fraudulent medical billing schemes, iPhone scams, credit card fraud, fake pop-up tech support scams, fraudulent car history report scams. They knew exactly how the money moved, and they invited him to open his own office and join them.

There was a second door too — a path to Dubai, into the world of crypto influencers. Pump-and-dump token launches. Posing in front of fake luxury — rented cars, rented apartments, rented watches — to convince strangers that a worthless coin was the opportunity of a lifetime. He had already seen exactly how this machine worked. He had been a victim of it. Now he was being invited to operate it.

He had every justification a person could need. He had been destroyed by people exactly like this. No one would have blamed him for becoming one of them. And for a moment, in the quiet of his lowest point, he considered it.

Then he thought past his own pain. He thought about the person on the other end of that phone call — someone's elderly mother, someone's tired father, someone who trusted a voice on the line the same way he had once trusted a woman who said she loved him. Why is it right to steal from someone? Why is it right to ruin another person's life for my own safety, my own recovery, my own needs? They don't owe me that.

He said no.

In his darkest moment — with $150,000 gone, three suicide attempts behind him, his family unaware of any of it, the people who once respected him now laughing — Arshad Noor made the choice that most people, even those who were never hurt this way, would not have the character to make. He chose not to become what hurt him. He chose to build instead.

That refusal became a decision to fight back instead. He had seen, up close, how international romance scam networks operate — many running out of Cambodia, China, Vietnam, and Dubai — deliberately targeting the most vulnerable: seniors, the lonely, the trusting. He had watched people fall for romance scams, tech support scams, lottery scams, the "grandson in trouble" scam — new variations appearing every single day. He chose the harder path: build something honest, slowly, instead of something fast and stolen. Not because it was easier. Because it let him sleep at night, and because people who knew the truth could still respect what he did.

That choice is not a marketing line. It is the entire moral foundation of Elysium Care. Every service, every policy, every person hired, every scam survivor supported for free — exists because of the decision made in that specific moment. You cannot fake that kind of integrity. It has to be earned the hardest possible way.
